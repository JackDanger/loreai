### Decision

<!-- lore:019e596c-e3a8-7229-bca2-92c0fe79e268 -->
* **Budget throttling uses proxy sleep / gradual slowdown, not hard 429 blocks**: Budget throttling uses proxy sleep (invisible delay) not hard 429 blocks. Rationale: flat 60s/120s Retry-After rejected as 'super annoying'; 429 triggers unpredictable client retry behavior; proxy sleep is invisible — upstream just appears slower; 3-5s sleep imperceptible during normal coding turn. Only conversation turns (Case 3 in pipeline.ts) throttled; meta requests and compaction exempt. Background workers excluded — their fraction of total costs is not meaningful to throttle. The throttle exploits gateway-level cross-request visibility to apply gentle friction without triggering client retry machinery.

### Gotcha

<!-- lore:019e5993-5e95-77cb-86e4-e5cb3ff76d4d -->
* **Budget throttle proxy sleep must be capped to avoid cache TTL busts**: Trap: injecting a proxy sleep for budget throttling looks safe because it's invisible to the client. But if the sleep pushes total request time past the cache TTL boundary (5m default, 1h upgraded), the next user turn arrives at a cold cache — causing a cache bust that costs MORE than the throttle saved, making the budget situation worse. Fix: cap the sleep at 50% of the remaining cache TTL window. \`sessionState.resolvedConversationTTL\` is available at the interception point (pipeline.ts ~line 3391); compute \`elapsed = now - lastRequestTime\`, \`remaining = ttlMs - elapsed\`, \`maxSleep = remaining \* 0.5\`. If cache is already expired, sleep = 0. This prevents the throttle from being self-defeating.
